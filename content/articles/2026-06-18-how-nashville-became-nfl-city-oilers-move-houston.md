## The Move Nobody Cleaned Up Neatly

Bud Adams didn't leave Houston because Nashville was ready. He left Houston because he was furious — at city hall, at the lease, at the Astrodome deal that never materialized the way he wanted. The destination almost didn't matter. That's the part people forget.

Nashville wasn't chosen. Nashville competed. And the distinction matters if you want to understand what kind of NFL city this actually became.

## Memphis Said No First

The short version most fans know: Adams moved the Oilers in 1997, they played two seasons in Memphis as a waystation, then landed in Nashville for 1999 as the Titans.

The longer version is messier. Memphis offered Liberty Bowl Memorial Stadium — a college facility that held roughly 62,000 people and had all the NFL infrastructure of a mid-size county fair. Tennessee Governor Don Sundquist and Nashville Mayor Phil Bredesen drove the actual deal. The state put $292 million toward what would become Adelphia Coliseum. Memphis got two lame-duck seasons worth of Oilers football. Nashville got a franchise.

That's a real negotiation. Not a gift.

## What the Temporary Oilers Showed Us

Those Memphis years (1997–1998) get treated as a footnote. They shouldn't be. The team went 8-8 in 1997 and 8-8 in 1998 playing in front of crowds that were half-empty and half-Houston transplants still wearing powder blue.

Steve McNair was already the quarterback. Eddie George was already running. Jeff Fisher was already building something. The roster that would make the Super Bowl in January 2000 was assembling itself inside a stadium where nobody was particularly sure who they were rooting for.

That tension — stateless, identity-less, caught between the franchise they left and the city they hadn't arrived in yet — actually forged something. Teams that know they're unwanted either collapse or get mean. That team got mean.

## Nashville Had to Show Up

When the Titans finally played at Adelphia in 1999, the city responded. Sellouts. Waiting lists. A 13-3 regular season that ended with the Music City Miracle — a lateral play that beat Buffalo and became the franchise's founding myth.

But the miracle only lands if the city was already invested. Nashville was.

The lesson isn't that NFL cities are made by owners writing checks or governors cutting deals. They're made by the years before the ribbon-cutting — the anger, the transit pain, the two years of football played in front of somebody else's fanbase.

Houston didn't lose a franchise because Adams was greedy. Houston lost a franchise because it failed to make the case.

Nashville made the case. That's still worth something.
