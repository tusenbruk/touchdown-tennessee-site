## The Number Was Never the Point

Derrick Henry rushed for 2,027 yards in 2020. Most people stopped there. They counted the yards, marveled at the highlights, moved on.

That's the wrong read.

The yards mattered. But what mattered more was the decision the Titans made every single week to hand him the ball again. And again. And again. In a league sprinting toward analytics and pass-first everything, Tennessee planted its flag in the dirt.

They bet on a running back. On purpose.

## What Vrabel Was Actually Saying

Mike Vrabel doesn't make speeches about identity. He just calls the play.

But every time offensive coordinator Arthur Smith handed Henry the ball on third-and-short instead of scheming a quick out, that was a statement. Every time they ran Henry at a stacked box in the fourth quarter — not because they had to, but because they *wanted* to — that was doctrine.

The Titans were telling the league something. We are not scared of you. We are going to run directly at you until you stop us.

Nobody stopped him.

## The Ryan Tannehill Factor

Here's what gets missed. Henry's season only works because Tannehill was good enough to keep defenses honest.

Tannehill completed 65 percent of his passes. He didn't turn the ball over. He was efficient. That's all the Titans needed from that position.

This was a conscious construction. Not an accident. Jon Robinson built a team around a bruising back and a game-manager quarterback and it nearly worked at the highest level. They went 11-5. They won the AFC South.

The front office gets credit they rarely receive for that blueprint.

## What It Said About Who They Are

Every franchise has an identity crisis eventually. The Titans had theirs in the mid-2010s — scrambling between quarterbacks, trying to be something slick, losing anyway.

Henry's 2020 season was the answer to all of that.

They stopped trying to be the Rams. They stopped chasing the Chiefs. They looked at what they had — a 247-pound freight train at running back, a tough offensive line, a defense built on physicality — and they said: *this is us.*

There's a lesson in that beyond football. Know what you are. Don't apologize for it.

## The Question That Remains

Henry left. Went to Baltimore. Won a Super Bowl in a different uniform.

The Titans have been searching for that identity ever since.

Maybe Will Levis gives them a new one. Maybe he doesn't. But what Henry's 2020 showed is that identity has to be built, not borrowed.

You don't find out who you are by watching someone else's film.
