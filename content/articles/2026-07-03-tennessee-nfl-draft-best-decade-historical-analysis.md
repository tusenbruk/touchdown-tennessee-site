## The Number That Keeps Coming Up

Peyton Manning left in 1998. The world has not stopped reminding us.

But here's the thing about the 1990s draft class from Tennessee — Manning is doing a lot of heavy lifting for that decade. Pull him out and you're left with a solid but unremarkable collection of second-rounders and depth guys. Reggie White was an '84 pick. Al Wilson went in '99. Good players. Not a dynasty.

The decade that actually holds up is the 2000s, and nobody talks about it that way.

## What the 2000s Actually Built

Between 2000 and 2009, Tennessee produced Donte Stallworth, John Henderson, Albert Haynesworth, Jason Witten, Marcus Spears, Jonathan Crompton — wait, not Crompton. But Witten in 2003. Haynesworth in 2002. Henderson in 2002. Rien Long won the Outland in 2003 and went first round.

That's three first-rounders in a two-year window, all from a program that was in the middle of its post-Fulmer transition wobble.

Witten played 17 seasons. Haynesworth, when he felt like it, was the most dominant defensive tackle in the league. Henderson started for Jacksonville for a decade.

The 2000s class doesn't get a monument because it didn't have a singular figure. It had a waterline. Sustained production at multiple positions across multiple years.

## What the 1990s Actually Had

The '90s had Manning and the mythology around Manning. It had Tee Martin winning a national championship and going nowhere in the NFL. It had Peerless Price, who had one great playoff catch and then kind of dissolved.

The decade produced stars, but it produced them in clusters around one era — 1995 to 1999 — and then mostly stopped. Peyton inflates the whole thing retrospectively.

## The 1980s Deserve a Second Look

Reggie White. Curt Wilkerson. Dale Jones. The '80s Tennessee pipeline was quietly consistent. No single transcendent player after White, but round after round of guys who stuck on rosters, started games, earned paychecks for eight years in the league.

That's not glamorous. It is, however, how you measure a program.

## The Real Question

If you're ranking decades by peak, the '90s win on the strength of one man.

If you're ranking by depth — by how many players from that decade started meaningful NFL games for meaningful stretches — the 2000s are closer than anyone admits, and the '80s are quietly in the conversation.

Tennessee fans remember Manning. NFL scouts remember Henderson playing 147 games for Jacksonville.

Both things are true. Only one of them tells you something about the program.
