## The Problem With Legends

Mike Munchak walked into the Titans' head coaching job in 2011 carrying something most coaches never get — automatic credibility. Nine Pro Bowls. Hall of Fame jacket. Fifteen seasons in the trenches making the Oilers and Titans respectable organizations. Nobody questioned whether he belonged in the building.

That was exactly the problem.

## The Halo That Blurred Everything

When you're that kind of legend, people see what they want to see. A 9-7 record in 2011 looked like promise. It was actually Jake Locker and Chris Johnson buying time on borrowed confidence. The roster had Bud Adams money but no coherent vision at quarterback — and a head coach who'd never called a play, never managed a clock, never stared down an owner demanding answers after a four-game skid.

Munchak was a position coach elevated by reverence, not by preparation for the full job.

He finished 22-26 in three seasons. Never made the playoffs. Got fired after a 2013 campaign where the offense finished 27th in the league. The organization gave him every chance to succeed, and the honest truth is he never found his footing as a chief executive of a football team.

## What the Film Really Shows

Here's the part nobody says out loud: being a transcendent player at one position does not translate into understanding the geometry of an entire roster. Munchak knew offensive line football the way a master sculptor knows marble. But running an NFL franchise requires you to think in 53-man ecosystems, in salary cap curves, in defensive coordinator hires you'll be judged for four years later.

He wasn't incompetent. That's too harsh and too simple. He was miscast.

The proof came after. He went back to what he was actually built to do — coaching offensive lines — and became one of the best position coaches in the NFL. Pittsburgh. Denver. He made guys better. He won assistant coach honors. He belonged in that room.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Tennessee gave Munchak the head job because of what he represented, not because of what he'd demonstrated as a coach. That's a front office failure dressed up as a loyalty move.

The Hall of Fame bust in Canton is fully deserved. The 22 wins in Nashville are a reminder that the game doesn't grant extensions for past glory.

The question worth asking now: how many teams are making the same mistake today, handing the keys to the franchise to someone whose greatness lived in one room?
