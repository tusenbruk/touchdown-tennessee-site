## The Banner They Forgot to Read

The welcome banner at Neyland Stadium in 1977 said JOHNNY MAJORS IS COMING HOME. Nobody thought much about the word *home*. They should have.

Majors left Pittsburgh as a national champion. He had just won the 1976 title with Tony Dorsett and a Panther team that ran over everybody. He could have stayed. He chose Tennessee instead. That detail gets glossed over every time someone tells this story.

He didn't have to come back. He wanted to.

## What He Found

Bill Battle left him a program that had gone 7-3-2 in 1976 and felt further away from a championship than that record suggested. The roster had talent in spots. The culture had drifted. Majors spent his first two seasons installing his wishbone and losing more than Tennessee fans were used to losing.

They booed him in Knoxville in 1978. He went 5-5-1.

He didn't quit. He didn't threaten to leave. He just kept recruiting.

## The Decade He Built

By 1985 he had Reggie White's successor, had Daryl Dickey running the offense, and had a program that finished 9-1-2 and won the SEC. By 1989 he was coaching Sterling Hinton and a defense that allowed 11 points a game. That team went 11-1.

Thirteen years in, and he had done the thing almost no coach does — rebuilt a program from the inside without burning it down first.

Then he had a heart attack in 1992.

## The Second Return Nobody Talks About Honestly

Phil Fulmer ran the team while Majors recovered. Tennessee went 4-1. When Majors came back, the locker room had shifted in a way you can't fully explain to someone who hasn't coached. The players had responded to Fulmer. The administration had noticed.

Majors coached out the season. He went 9-3. The school removed him anyway.

The official language was polite. The actual dynamic was not complicated. A man came back from a heart attack to find that the institution he had chosen over a national championship in Pittsburgh had already started moving on without him.

He was 57.

## What That Means

Johnny Majors is remembered as the man who rebuilt Tennessee football. That is accurate. He is also the story of what happens when a program outgrows the person who grew it.

Neyland Stadium holds over 100,000 people now. Majors is part of why that number means anything.

The question worth sitting with: did Tennessee know what it had before it let it go, or did it only figure that out at the funeral in 2020?
