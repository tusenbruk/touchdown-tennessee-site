## The Part Nobody Talks About

Bud Adams was not beloved when he left Houston. Oiler fans burned his jersey. They called him a traitor. They were right, and none of that matters now.

What matters is what he built after the wreckage.

People treat the move to Tennessee like a business decision. It was. But business decisions don't explain why Adams kept this franchise together through two stadium limbo years in Memphis, through embarrassing crowds at the Liberty Bowl, through a fanbase that hadn't yet decided if it wanted him.

He stayed. That's the part worth examining.

## The Gamble Nobody Remembers

Adams didn't move to a sure thing. Nashville wasn't a football town in 1997. It was a country music town with a new arena and a lot of civic optimism. The NFL sold it as a market. Adams sold it to himself as a legacy.

He was 74 years old when this team played its first game in Tennessee. Men his age don't usually bet everything on a city still learning its name.

He hired Jeff Fisher. He kept Jeff Fisher when it would've been easier to fire him. He drafted Steve McNair and let McNair become Steve McNair — which required patience most owners don't have.

## What the Super Bowl Run Actually Proves

The 1999 season didn't happen because of a genius front office. It happened because Adams had built something with enough stability to catch fire.

The Music City Miracle doesn't exist without a quarterback who'd been developed for years. The 16-15 Super Bowl loss doesn't happen without a roster built carefully, not frantically.

Adams made bad decisions too. Personnel. Coaches. Late-career stubbornness. The record shows it.

But he also made the whole thing possible. That's not a small thing.

## What Tennessee Owes Him

This state had college football. It didn't have professional football until one oil man from Texas decided Nashville was worth the trouble.

The Titans have identity problems right now. New ownership. New coach. Young roster. The franchise is searching.

That's fine. Franchises search. The important thing is the franchise exists.

Adams died in 2013. The team he left behind has been trying to figure out what it is ever since.

Maybe that's the real question his era leaves us: he knew what he was building. Does anyone in that building know now?
