## The Pattern Nobody Wants to Name

The Tennessee Titans have drafted well enough to stay relevant and poorly enough to never become great. That's not an accident. That's a philosophy.

Since moving from Houston in 1997, this franchise has produced a handful of legitimate hits — Keith Bulluck, Kevin Byard, Derrick Henry in the fifth round — and a graveyard of first-round investments that went nowhere fast. What connects the misses isn't bad luck. It's a consistent reluctance to draft for ceiling.

## The Steve McNair Exception

The greatest Titan was drafted third overall in 1995, when the franchise was still the Oilers. McNair was a swing. A genuine swing. He was raw, he was risky, and he required patience. He became the heartbeat of an AFC championship era.

The Titans have not swung like that since. Not once.

## First-Round Risk Aversion

Look at the first-round selections from 2005 to 2015. Adam Jones. Vince Young — which was forced by fan pressure as much as front office conviction. Michael Griffin. Derrick Morgan. Jake Locker. Chance Warmack.

That is a list of players selected to not embarrass the front office. Safe. Projectable. Gradeable. The kind of picks that look defensible at the podium in April and forgettable by October.

Warmack at tenth overall in 2013 is the thesis statement. Interior offensive linemen don't go top ten unless you're so afraid of a boom-or-bust prospect that you reach for the most conservative option on the board.

## The Henry Anomaly

Derrick Henry in the fifth round is the great irony of this organization. The best player they've developed in twenty years slipped because the league thought he was a one-trick workhorse. The Titans took him and then — for two seasons — barely used him.

Even when they stumbled into greatness, they flinched.

## What Callie Matula and Jon Robinson Revealed

Robinson's tenure showed flashes of genuine conviction. Byard in the fifth round. Henry. A.J. Brown in the second. Those are value picks with belief behind them.

But the top of the draft remained cautious. Jeffery Simmons was the right call in 2019, but he came off a torn ACL and slid. The Titans didn't so much select him as catch him falling.

## The Honest Question

A franchise's draft history is its self-portrait. And what Tennessee's portrait shows is an organization that trusts the process more than the player, the grade more than the gut.

The next great Titan is probably sitting in a mid-round slot right now — waiting for someone in that war room to stop being afraid and just believe in the kid.

The question is whether this front office has that in them.
