## The Stadium Was Obsolete Before the Carpet Dried

Nissan Stadium opened in 1999 as Adelphia Coliseum — a name that aged about as well as the concrete it was stamped on. The city of Nashville issued $144 million in bonds to build it. The Titans signed a 30-year lease and called it home.

The problem is what "home" actually looked like. The stadium had a roof that leaked, seats that required binoculars from the upper deck, and a bathroom-to-fan ratio that turned every halftime into a hostage situation. None of that is hyperbole. Fan surveys consistently ranked Nissan among the league's worst game-day experiences. The NFL's own facility grades said the same.

This wasn't bad luck. It was design. The stadium was built to a 1990s budget with a 1990s vision of what an NFL venue should be. By the time the Titans were making their Super Bowl XXXIV run in January 2000 — one yard short, Kevin Dyson frozen at the five — the building was already behind the curve.

## Twenty-Five Years of Duct Tape

The franchise spent the next two decades doing what teams do with aging infrastructure: patching. New video boards in 2014. Wi-Fi upgrades. Club-level renovations. They kept slapping new siding on a house with a cracked foundation.

Meanwhile, Nashville transformed into one of the fastest-growing cities in America. The skyline across the Cumberland River went from three notable buildings to fifteen. The city added the Predators, an MLS franchise, a thriving entertainment economy. Nissan Stadium sat there looking like a parking garage someone forgot to finish.

The structural assessment released in 2022 wasn't a surprise to anyone who had sat through a November rain in Section 330. The report found significant deterioration. Repairing the existing structure would cost nearly as much as building new. The math wrote itself.

## What the New Stadium Actually Represents

The $2.1 billion domed stadium — now under construction with a targeted 2027 opening — isn't just an upgrade. It's Nashville finally admitting what it wants to be.

This city hosts the NFL Draft. It hosts major concerts, college football, international soccer. A fixed-roof stadium in a Tennessee August was always a negotiating weakness. A retractable-roof venue changes the conversation with every event promoter in North America.

The Titans get a building worth playing in. Nashville gets an anchor for the East Bank development that city planners have been eyeing for a decade. The old stadium gets demolished, which is the most honest thing anyone can say about it.

Twenty-five years is a long time to wait for a building that should have been better from day one. At least they finally stopped waiting.
