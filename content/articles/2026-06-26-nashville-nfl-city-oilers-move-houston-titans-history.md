## The Scoreboard Nobody Talks About

Bud Adams watched his stadium crumble and his crowds shrink, and he did the math.

Houston had the Astrodome — once the Eighth Wonder of the World, then just a tired, leaking concrete bowl that smelled like the 1970s. By the mid-90s, the city of Houston would not build Adams a new one. Harris County politicians had no appetite for it. The fans, distracted by the Rockets winning championships and the Cowboys existing ninety miles away, had checked out.

The Oilers averaged fewer than 30,000 fans per game in 1995. In a stadium that seated 62,000.

That's not a franchise leaving a city. That's a city leaving a franchise first.

## Tennessee Wanted It Ugly

Nashville didn't seduce Adams with some slick presentation. Tennessee Governor Don Sundquist and Nashville Mayor Phil Bredesen made an aggressive play — public financing, a new stadium on the Cumberland River, and a market hungry for major-league legitimacy.

But here's what people forget: the move happened in stages deliberately designed to humiliate everyone involved.

The Oilers played 1996 in Memphis. *Memphis.* As a placeholder. In the Liberty Bowl. Drawing 30,000 fans per game — almost identical to what they drew in Houston — proving the problem was never geography. It was the product.

Then came 1997, when they played home games in Vanderbilt Stadium while the new riverfront palace got built. College bleachers. A temporary NFL franchise in a college yard.

And people *still* showed up.

## What Nashville Was Actually Buying

This is the part the NFL marketing people won't frame for you.

Nashville wasn't buying a football team. Nashville was buying its seat at the adult table.

In 1997, this city was a country music town on the edge of an identity explosion. Young professionals were arriving. The healthcare industry was growing. The city was changing faster than its infrastructure could handle. An NFL franchise wasn't a luxury — it was a civic argument. It said: *we are a major American city now, and we have the lease to prove it.*

The Oilers became the Titans in 1999. Won the AFC Championship that same year. Steve McNair and Eddie George ran into the end zone and took an entire region's self-image with them.

## The Question Worth Asking

Houston eventually got another team — the Texans, in 2002, in a brand new stadium the city finally agreed to build.

All it took was losing their team to wake them up.

Which makes you wonder: did Nashville build the Titans, or did Houston's indifference do it for them?
