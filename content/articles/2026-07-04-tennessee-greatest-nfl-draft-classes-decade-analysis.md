## The Wrong Decade Gets the Credit

Peyton Manning left in 1998. That number sits so heavy on Tennessee football history that it bends everything around it. Ask a casual fan which era produced the most NFL talent from Knoxville and they'll say the late '90s. They're not wrong about the players. They're wrong about the decade.

The 1990s gets the trophy case. The 2000s built the factory.

## What the 2000s Actually Did

Between 2000 and 2009, Tennessee sent 57 players to NFL rosters. Not drafted — rostered. That's a different number and a harder one to fake.

Albert Haynesworth went in the fifth round in 2002. Fifth. He became one of the most disruptive interior linemen of his generation before the contract and the controversy ate him. Jevon Kearse had already arrived, but his best NFL years — the ones where opposing offensive coordinators lost sleep — came in that first half of the decade.

Then add Jason Witten. Marcus Spears. David Greene never panned out, but he went. Kevin Burnett. Parys Haralson. Robert Ayers. These aren't household names. That's exactly the point.

## Sustained vs. Spectacular

The 1990s class — Peyton, Tee Martin, Peerless Price, Al Wilson, Leonard Little — produced more individual star power. Wilson anchored a Denver defense. Little played 11 seasons despite everything. Price had that Super Bowl.

But sustained production across a full decade? Across multiple rounds, multiple positions, multiple coaching staffs? That's the 2000s, and it's not close.

Phil Fulmer was running a professional pipeline whether anyone called it that or not. His offensive line recruiting alone — look at the guys who spent years holding NFL rosters together without ever making a Pro Bowl. That's the invisible column in the ledger.

## The Decade Nobody Talks About

The 1980s deserve a sentence. Reggie White alone makes it competitive. Fuad Reveiz. Dale Jones. The '80s had a ceiling. Reggie White is the ceiling. But the floor was low and the volume wasn't there.

The 2000s had depth. Rounds 1 through 7. Practice squads that became starters. Late picks that outlasted early ones.

## What It Actually Means

This isn't about rankings. It's about what a program is actually doing beneath the famous names.

Tennessee in the 2000s was developing players the NFL wanted even when the scoreboard in Knoxville was ugly. That's program-level work. That's worth noting.

The question now is whether the current roster — with Nico Collins already gone, with this wave of transfer talent — can build something similar.

Or whether it stays spectacular without ever becoming sustained.
