## The Pattern Nobody Wants to Name

Fear drafts like a fingerprint. You can see it in the choices.

The Tennessee Titans have made safe picks for twenty-five years. Not bad picks — safe ones. There's a difference, and that difference is why this franchise has one Super Bowl appearance and a Music City Miracle that people remember more than anything that came after it.

Look at the history and a pattern emerges that nobody in the building wants to say out loud: the Titans draft for floor, not ceiling. They take the guy who won't embarrass anybody. They take the guy who fits the scheme. They almost never take the guy who could break the league.

## The Hits That Prove the Point

Kevin Byard in 2016. A third-round pick out of Middle Tennessee State who became an All-Pro. Classic Titans logic — a safe, scheme-fit pick that outperformed expectations because the player was genuinely exceptional.

Derrick Henry wasn't a first-round pick. He fell to 45. The Titans didn't believe in him enough to take him early. He dragged this franchise to an AFC Championship game through sheer force of will.

The hits here aren't the result of bold vision. They're accidents of value.

## The Misses That Sting Hardest

Jake Locker at eight overall in 2011. A quarterback the Titans talked themselves into because he was athletic and local and safe-feeling. He started 22 games. Twenty-two.

The Titans sat at the top of the draft in 2016 and traded down from first overall. They passed on Jared Goff and Carson Wentz. They took Jack Conklin — a good player, a very good player — and watched Wentz go to a Super Bowl.

That trade-down philosophy is the Titans in a sentence. We'll get value. We'll be responsible. We won't swing and miss.

## What It Actually Reveals

A front office that drafts this way is a front office that grades itself on process, not outcomes. They want to be able to explain every pick. They want the tape to justify the selection. They want cover.

That's not scouting. That's corporate risk management.

The franchises that win — Kansas City, San Francisco, Philadelphia — they take swings. They draft players who might not fit. They bet on traits and upside and occasionally look completely wrong for two years before they look completely right.

The Titans have drafted like a team trying not to get fired.

## The Question Worth Asking

With a new regime now holding the pen, the real question isn't whether they'll draft well.

It's whether they'll finally be willing to be wrong.
