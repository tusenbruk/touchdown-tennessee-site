## The Hire That Changes Simmons' Ceiling

Robert Saleh built his reputation on one thing: making defensive linemen into monsters. Ask Nick Bosa. Ask Arik Armstead. Now ask what happens when that coaching brain gets his hands on Jeffery Simmons.

Simmons posted the best numbers of his career in 2025 — interior pressure rate that ranked top-five among 4-3 tackles, consistent double-team absorption that freed up linebackers to make plays in space. That was without a coordinator who specifically engineers systems to exploit those traits. Saleh doesn't just use his best defenders. He builds the scheme around them.

## What Saleh Actually Does

Saleh's defenses in San Francisco and New York weren't complicated. They were disciplined. He runs a wide-9 front with an emphasis on one-gap penetration — exactly the assignment that lets a physical freak like Simmons operate downhill instead of two-gapping and absorbing blocks all afternoon.

In Saleh's system in San Francisco, interior linemen averaged 8.2% more pressures than the league average at their position. That's not a coincidence. It's scheme design.

Simmons attacking a one-gap assignment, with the edges collapsing the pocket toward him, is a genuinely uncomfortable thing for an offensive coordinator to draw up on Sunday morning.

## The Week 1 Stakes

Tennessee opens September 13th at home against the Jets — a team Saleh coached for four years. He knows their personnel tendencies cold. He knows which protection schemes they lean on under pressure, which backs they trust in pass protection, where the vulnerabilities are.

DraftKings has the Titans at -3, O/U sitting at 39.5. That's a low total, which suggests the market respects what this Tennessee defense could be. The moneyline opened at -170 and has settled to -162 — modest movement, but the direction matters.

A tight, low-scoring Week 1 win at Nissan Stadium is exactly the kind of game Saleh-coached defenses are built to manufacture.

## The Question That Follows Him

Saleh's Jets defenses never quite matched his San Francisco work. Personnel matters. New York didn't give him the front-four talent to execute the vision consistently. Tennessee has Simmons. That's a different starting point.

The real test isn't Week 1. It's whether Saleh can sustain the scheme discipline through October and November when teams have had time to study his tendencies and counter.

But right now, heading into a season opener, the Titans have a defensive-minded head coach, a legitimate superstar at defensive tackle, and a home crowd ready to see what this thing looks like.

If Simmons finishes 2026 as a Defensive Player of the Year candidate, nobody should act surprised. Saleh has done this before.
