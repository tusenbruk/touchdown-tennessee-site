## The Number Nobody Talks About

Eddie George carried the ball 403 times in 2000. Four hundred and three times. The Titans finished 13-3 that season. Those two facts are not unrelated.

We remember George for the highlight reel — the stiff arm that sent a linebacker into the turf, the third-and-short conversion where he simply refused to go down. But the thing that defined him wasn't any single carry. It was the cumulative weight of all of them. He absorbed punishment the way a foundation absorbs load. Everything above him stayed standing.

## What Ohio State Sent to Nashville

George won the 1995 Heisman at Ohio State and arrived in Houston — then Tennessee — as a finished product. Big back, textbook vision, legitimate speed. The scouting report looked clean. What the scouting report couldn't measure was his tolerance for irrelevance.

The Oilers were becoming the Titans. The franchise was mid-identity-crisis. They played a season in Memphis. They played in the Liberty Bowl. George showed up for that too. He ran the ball in front of crowds that had no reason to love the team yet. That matters. That's not nothing.

## The Jeff Fisher System and What It Required

Fisher ran a grind offense. Two tight ends, physical line, ground-and-pound until the defense bent. It was not a system designed to make a running back famous. It was designed to win by 10 in January.

George averaged 14.7 carries per game across his Titans career. He eclipsed 1,000 yards six straight seasons. But more useful than the yardage totals is where those yards came from — inside zone, between the tackles, first and second down, when everyone in the stadium knew the ball was going to him. He still got four yards.

That kind of running requires something closer to discipline than athleticism.

## Super Bowl XXXIV and the Play Before the Play

The Rams won Super Bowl XXXIV. Kevin Dyson came one yard short on the last play. Everyone knows that.

Fewer people remember that George carried 28 times for 95 yards in that game against the best defense the Rams had to offer. He kept the Titans in it long enough for the finish to be miraculous rather than inevitable. The lateral to McNair that set up the final drive? George had already earned every inch of the field position that made it possible.

## What He Actually Was

George wasn't the most explosive back of his era. Marshall Faulk, Jamal Lewis, LaDainian Tomlinson — the conversation at peak gets complicated fast.

But no back of that generation did more to legitimize a franchise still finding its footing. He was the proof of concept. If Eddie George is here and he's running hard, this thing is real.

Sometimes that's the most important job on the roster.
