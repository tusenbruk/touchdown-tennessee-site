## The Wrong Question

Everybody asks how many yards he got. That's the wrong question.

Eddie George ran for 10,441 yards in a Titans uniform. Fine. Write it down. Now forget it for a minute. Because the number misses the point the same way a box score misses a war.

The real question is what he meant to a franchise that was still learning its own name.

## A City That Needed Someone to Follow

The Oilers moved to Nashville in 1997. Nobody here asked for them. The locals were suspicious. The players felt orphaned. The identity was a blank page.

George filled it.

He won the Heisman at Ohio State in '95. He could've been a symbol of somewhere else forever. Instead he became the face of something that didn't exist yet. He ran like a man who believed the whole thing was real before anyone else did.

That matters. Cities don't adopt teams. They adopt players. George made Nashville adopt the Titans.

## What Three Yards Looked Like

Here's what people forget about Eddie George. He wasn't elusive. He wasn't a burner. He was 6-foot-3, 240 pounds, and he ran directly at problems.

Third and two. Stacked box. George took the handoff and dared the defense to stop him.

Sometimes they did. He got up. He came back. He did it again.

That stubbornness was a philosophy. The Titans of that era played like they had something to prove every single Sunday. George set the tempo for all of it. Steve McNair got the glory. George laid the foundation McNair ran on.

## The Music City Miracle Had His Fingerprints

People remember the lateral. Kevin Dyson. January 2000. History.

What they gloss over is the 16-game season that got the Titans to that moment. George carried the ball 403 times that year. Four hundred and three times. He absorbed punishment that would've ended most careers and kept the offense moving when nothing else worked.

The miracle doesn't happen without the grind. George was the grind.

## What He Left Behind

George retired after the 2004 season. The Titans got worse almost immediately. That's correlation, not causation — but it's hard to ignore.

He didn't just leave a rushing record. He left a standard. A way of showing up. A definition of what a Titan was supposed to look like on a cold Sunday in December when the game meant something.

This franchise is still searching for that guy.

Maybe they always will be.
