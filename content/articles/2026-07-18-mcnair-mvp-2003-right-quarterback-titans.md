## The Number Everyone Misremembers

Steve McNair threw 24 touchdowns and 7 interceptions in 2003. Good numbers. Not absurd numbers. Peyton Manning, who shared the MVP trophy with him that year, threw 29 touchdowns. The box score argument favors Peyton. It almost always did.

But football isn't a box score argument. And McNair's value to that specific Titans team was something that doesn't fit in a column.

## What That Offense Actually Was

Jeff Fisher ran a grind-it-out system built around Eddie George's replacement — a young Chris Brown — and a line that wanted to control clock and field position. The passing game existed to punish defenses for loading the box. That's not an insult. That's a specific, intentional design.

McNair's job wasn't to carry the offense. His job was to make defenses pay for stopping the run, and to survive long enough to do it.

He took 46 sacks that season. Forty-six. He played through a partially torn sternoclavicular joint, a bruised sternum, and a bad knee. He missed one start. The Titans went 12-4.

## The Specific Thing He Did

What McNair gave Fisher's team was a quarterback who functioned like a second run blocker when the play broke down. He absorbed hits on scrambles that other quarterbacks would have slid away from or thrown away. Third-and-4 in the fourth quarter, McNair tucked it and got you the first down. He didn't protect his stats. He protected the drive.

Against the Ravens in Week 9, he converted three third downs with his legs in the fourth quarter alone. Baltimore was playing zone specifically to limit his arm. He didn't care.

## Why the Fit Was the Point

A drop-back pocket passer on that team struggles. The line wasn't a pass-protection unit. It was a mauling unit. You needed someone who could buy time with his feet and make a decision at the last possible second without flinching.

McNair processed fast and held the ball late. That combination is either reckless or brilliant depending on the quarterback. For him, it was the entire mechanism.

## The Honest Takeaway

People remember the shared trophy as some kind of consolation prize — like the voters split the difference between Peyton's numbers and McNair's narrative. That's wrong. The voters saw a 12-4 team built around a quarterback who made every hard yard count.

Peyton Manning had a better arm. Steve McNair was a better fit.

Sometimes those are the same thing. In 2003, they weren't.
